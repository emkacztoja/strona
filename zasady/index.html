F1 RULES & REGULATIONS

UNDERSTANDING THE SPORT'S FRAMEWORK

THE RULEBOOK

FORMULA 1'S COMPREHENSIVE REGULATIONS

Formula 1 is governed by a complex set of regulations that dictate everything from car design to race procedures. These rules ensure fair competition, maintain safety standards, and promote technological innovation within defined parameters.

The regulations are updated annually by the FIA (Fédération Internationale de l'Automobile), the sport's governing body, in consultation with teams, drivers, and other stakeholders.

TECHNICAL REGULATIONS

CAR SPECIFICATIONS & REQUIREMENTS

Car Components

The F1 car must adhere to strict dimensions: maximum width of 2000mm, maximum length of 5600mm. Weight requirements mandate a minimum of 798kg including the driver. Power units consist of a 1.6-liter V6 turbo hybrid with energy recovery systems.

Learn More

Aerodynamics

Stringent regulations control aerodynamic components, including front and rear wings, floor design, and diffuser dimensions. Ground effect aerodynamics were reintroduced in 2022 to facilitate closer racing while reducing "dirty air" effects.

Learn More

Safety Features

Mandatory safety elements include the halo cockpit protection system, survival cell construction, impact structures, and fire suppression systems. Crash tests must be passed before a car is deemed race-worthy.

SPORTING REGULATIONS

COMPETITION RULES & PROCEDURES

Race Weekend Format

A standard race weekend consists of three practice sessions (FP1, FP2, FP3), qualifying, and the race. Sprint weekends feature a modified format with only one practice session before Sprint Qualifying.

Qualifying System

Qualifying is divided into three segments: Q1 (18 minutes, eliminating 5 slowest cars), Q2 (15 minutes, eliminating 5 more cars), and Q3 (12 minutes for the top 10 to determine pole position).

Engine & Component Allocation

Drivers are limited to using 3 Internal Combustion Engines, 3 Turbochargers, 3 MGU-H units, 3 MGU-K units, 2 Energy Stores, 2 Control Electronics sets, and 8 exhaust systems per season.

Parc Fermé Rules

Once cars enter qualifying, they enter "parc fermé" conditions where significant changes to car setup are prohibited until the race begins. This ensures cars qualify and race in the same specification.

Championships

Formula 1 awards two championships each season:

  • Drivers' Championship: Awarded to the driver who scores the most points across the season.
  • Constructors' Championship: Awarded to the team whose drivers score the most combined points across the season.

FLAG SIGNALS

COMMUNICATION ON TRACK

Red Flag

Session stopped. All cars must return to pit lane immediately at reduced speed.

Yellow Flag

Danger ahead. Slow down, no overtaking until past the green flag.

Red & Yellow Striped Flag

Slippery surface ahead, typically due to oil, water, or debris.

Blue Flag

Faster car approaching to lap you. Let them pass at the earliest opportunity.

Black & White Flag

Warning for unsportsmanlike behavior. Shown with car number.

Black Flag

Driver disqualified. Must return to pits immediately. Shown with car number.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

COMMON QUERIES ABOUT F1 RULES

What is DRS and how does it work?

+

DRS (Drag Reduction System) is an adjustable flap in the rear wing that reduces aerodynamic drag. Drivers can activate DRS in designated zones when they are within 1 second of the car ahead, helping to facilitate overtaking.

What happens if a race cannot be completed?

+

If a race is red-flagged and cannot be restarted, points are awarded based on how much of the race was completed: no points if less than 2 laps were completed; reduced points if less than 75% of the scheduled race distance was completed; full points if more than 75% was completed.

How are grid penalties applied?

+

Grid penalties are applied after qualifying in the order they were imposed. If a driver incurs more penalty places than their grid position allows (e.g., a driver qualifying 18th with a 20-place penalty), they start from the back. When multiple drivers have back-of-grid penalties, their order is determined by their qualifying positions.

What is the 107% rule in qualifying?

+

Any driver whose best qualifying lap exceeds 107% of the fastest Q1 time may not be allowed to race. However, stewards can permit them to start if they have shown satisfactory pace in practice sessions or under exceptional circumstances.
